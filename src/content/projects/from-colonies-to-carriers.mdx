## Overview

When African states achieved independence in the late 1950s and 1960s, establishing a national airline was rarely a matter of economic logic alone. An airline was a flag, a symbol of sovereignty, a daily assertion that the new state existed and belonged in the world. This project traces how those decisions were made, who made them, and what they cost — and what they reveal about postcolonial state-building more broadly.

## Case Study: Royal Air Maroc

The project centers on Royal Air Maroc (RAM) as a case study in postcolonial institution-building. Morocco nationalized its carrier in 1957 — just one year after independence from France — making RAM one of the earliest Africanized airlines on the continent. The airline's trajectory, from a jointly managed colonial concession to a sovereign flag carrier, mirrors patterns visible across the continent while also reflecting Morocco's particular political economy, its relationship with France, and the specific pressures of building a national institution from a colonial infrastructure.

RAM's history makes visible what most histories of Moroccan sovereignty skip: the slow institutional work of transformation, the negotiations over control, routes, and expertise, and the way a symbol of modernity could also be an instrument of geopolitical maneuvering.

## Comparative Framework

The broader research extends this analysis outward. Rather than treating RAM as a curiosity, the project situates it within a comparative dataset of African carrier development — tracking founding dates, fleet composition, route networks, ownership structures, and institutional trajectories across the continent's early post-independence decades.

This comparative layer asks a different question: was there a pattern? Did states that moved quickly to nationalize their carriers face different development constraints than those that maintained joint arrangements longer? Did Cold War alignment affect route access in measurable ways?

## Data Archaeology

A core methodological challenge for this project is source fragility. Civil aviation records from this period are scattered across national archives, ICAO documents, colonial administrative files, aviation trade press, and timetable ephemera. Many carriers that no longer exist left only partial traces — financial records destroyed, routes documented only in advertisement copy, fleet histories buried in industry directories.

The project involves systematic reconstruction: identifying what sources exist, cross-referencing discontinuous records, and building a structured dataset from fragmentary evidence. The goal is not completeness for its own sake, but a dataset legible enough to support genuine analysis — to move from "here is what we have" to "here is what the pattern suggests."

## Why It Matters

African airlines were built at a moment when the infrastructure of sovereignty was being assembled from scratch. Understanding how those decisions were made — and how they shaped later airline viability, route access, and state relationships with international carriers and lending institutions — still matters for current discussions about African aviation policy, continental integration, and the longer arc of postcolonial economic development.

The project also raises a methodological question worth taking seriously: what does it mean to write institutional history from fragmentary archives? How do you be honest about gaps while still making the evidence speak? These questions are not peripheral. They are central to the kind of digital history this project is trying to practice.

## Status

This project emerged from graduate seminar research at NC State University (HI 598) and is now being extended into a working digital dataset. Dataset reconstruction is ongoing; a working paper and public dataset are in preparation.
