## Overview

International work often gets described too loosely, as if travel alone were evidence of depth. The more serious question is what kind of attention a place teaches you to practice, and how that attention changes your work.

This project frame treats Morocco as a site of language learning, teaching, intercultural work, and research practice. It matters not because it sounds international, but because it sharpened Reese's ability to work across linguistic, disciplinary, and institutional boundaries in a sustained way during 2023-2024.

## Role

Reese's role here was inherently hybrid: researcher, language learner, teacher, and public-facing interpreter. Under a Fulbright U.S. Student Program grant, he moved among Arabic study, historical research, classroom teaching, and outward-facing academic communication without flattening any of them.

## What I did

The work demanded a layered kind of attentiveness. Language learning required humility and repetition. Reese studied Modern Standard Arabic and Moroccan Darija intensively in Rabat while also working across French and Moroccan institutional settings. Research required historical and political sensitivity, including work on the colonial history of tourism in Ifrane and the Atlas Mountains. Teaching demanded translation and audience awareness, whether in public-facing academic contexts or while teaching Modern U.S. History and student-oriented writing support in Morocco.

That combination is central to Reese's broader positioning. He is not simply interested in international topics; he is interested in what it takes to make them legible across differences in language, background, and institutional context.

## Why it matters

The clearest impact of this work is qualitative but durable. It strengthened Reese's cross-cultural judgment, sharpened his long-term focus on North Africa, and reinforced a public-facing approach to scholarship that takes explanation seriously.

It also helped make visible the portfolio's central throughline: the best work happens where research, teaching, and design sensibility all have to operate at once. The Morocco year also seeded later work in infrastructure, pedagogy, and public scholarship rather than standing apart from it.

- Translating academic material for broader audiences without draining out complexity
- Teaching and researching in intercultural settings
- Producing thoughtful public-facing scholarship from field-based experience
