## Overview

Most accounts of Western Sahara keep diplomatic recognition and physical infrastructure in separate stories. One story tracks resolutions, recognition signals, and institutional framing. The other tracks roads, ports, and southern development plans. The capstone's central wager was that those stories should be read together.

The project asked a causal question: how did physical integration, especially transport infrastructure, help create the conditions under which Morocco's sovereignty claims became easier to normalize?

## Role

Reese built the case study from the ground up. That meant source gathering, argument design, visual curation, and a lightweight data workflow for making the infrastructure side more concrete. The work was not just a written thesis question; it also required building a clearer evidentiary apparatus around it.

## What I did

The research combined three kinds of work.

First, Reese assembled and organized locally available primary and secondary materials into a usable source base. That included diplomatic documents, infrastructure reports, archival maps, and legal or institutional facsimiles.

Second, he built a reproducible roads-over-time workflow using OSM relation `R2559126` and the ohsome history API for year-end snapshots from 2008 through 2025. That workflow created a cleaner way to talk about infrastructure expansion without relying only on anecdote.

Third, he translated the whole argument into presentation-ready visual logic. The dual timeline was especially important because it forced the mechanism into view: diplomatic milestones on one axis, infrastructure milestones on the other, read together rather than sequentially in isolation.

## Why it matters

The capstone produced more than a claim. It yielded a reusable evidence set, a clear visual narrative for presentation, a reproducible roads-over-time workflow, and a more persuasive mechanism for explaining why the politics of sovereignty cannot be separated from the politics of circulation.

It also clarified Reese's own working style: he is strongest when argument, evidence design, and public explanation are treated as one problem rather than three separate ones.

## Skills

- Process tracing across diplomatic and infrastructural timelines
- Source synthesis across archival, statistical, and visual materials
- Designing explanatory figures that carry analytical weight
- Turning a research question into a public-facing, teachable case study
