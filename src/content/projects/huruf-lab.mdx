## Overview

Beginning Arabic learners are often asked to internalize a great deal at once: a new script, positional letter forms, sound distinctions, and the logic of roots and patterns. Much of that instruction remains abstract. Students are expected to "get it" by repeated exposure even when the system itself still feels invisible.

Huruf La'b starts from a different premise. If the structure is hard to see, it should become something a learner can handle, rearrange, and test physically. The project also responds to a teacher-side problem: many Arabic manipulatives are difficult to integrate into classroom routines, so they end up admired in theory and ignored in practice.

## Role

Reese helped frame the concept, translate pedagogical friction into design requirements, and treat early prototyping as a research problem rather than a branding exercise. The work sat at the overlap of instructional design, language learning, entrepreneurship, and product thinking.

## What I did

The first design move was diagnostic: identify which parts of early Arabic instruction remain stubbornly opaque. That meant focusing not only on the alphabet, but on the transition from isolated recognition to compositional understanding.

From there, the system was imagined as tactile and modular. Rather than presenting letters as inert symbols on a page, the kit makes them manipulable. Learners can externalize relationships between forms, practice recombination, and build a more embodied sense of how written Arabic behaves.

The product concept also had to avoid becoming a novelty item. That required keeping the pedagogical logic clear: each interaction needed to help a learner see structure, not just feel delighted by materials.

Customer discovery sharpened the design. Teacher outreach produced an early email list of more than 100 interested people, including at least two dozen Arabic teachers across the United States, the United Kingdom, and the Arab world. Those conversations pushed the project beyond the puzzle itself. Teachers repeatedly asked for scaffolding, short repeatable routines, clear instructions, and a fuller ecosystem that could include workbooks, video guides, and a phonics layer rather than a loose manipulative set.

## Why it matters

At this stage, the main outcome is a more credible instructional system concept: one that treats Arabic learning as a design problem with specific points of friction rather than as a generic ed-tech opportunity. The project now has clearer evidence about what adoption hinges on, better-defined classroom use cases, and a stronger rationale for building a full learning ecosystem rather than a standalone object.

Just as importantly, the project demonstrates a habit of mind that carries across Reese's work: when understanding stalls, redesign the conditions of understanding.
